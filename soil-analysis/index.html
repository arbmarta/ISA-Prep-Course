Soil Analysis

Learn how soil-testing results can help arborists evaluate growing conditions and make appropriate management decisions.

Learning Objectives

After completing this lesson, students should be able to:

  • Identify the major components of a soil-analysis report.
  • Interpret soil pH results.
  • Recognize the importance of soil texture and organic matter.
  • Evaluate reported nutrient levels.
  • Use soil-test information to support tree-care decisions.

Major Soil Characteristics

Soil pH

Soil pH describes how acidic or alkaline the soil is. It affects nutrient availability and biological activity.

Soil Texture

Soil texture describes the relative amounts of sand, silt, and clay in a soil sample.

Organic Matter

Organic matter can improve soil structure, water retention, nutrient-holding capacity, and biological activity.

Nutrient Levels

Soil tests may report levels of phosphorus, potassium, calcium, magnesium, and other essential elements.

Soluble Salts

High soluble-salt levels can interfere with water uptake and may damage tree roots.

Cation Exchange Capacity

Cation exchange capacity, or CEC, indicates a soil’s ability to hold positively charged nutrient ions.

Soil Characteristic Result Interpretation
pH 7.8 Moderately alkaline
Organic matter 2.1% Relatively low
Phosphorus 18 ppm Interpret using the laboratory’s reference range
Potassium 145 ppm Interpret using the laboratory’s reference range
Soluble salts 0.7 dS/m Review in relation to tree species and site conditions

Important Reminder

Soil-test numbers should not be interpreted in isolation. Recommendations should also consider the tree species, observed symptoms, site history, soil conditions, root environment, and the testing laboratory’s reference ranges.
